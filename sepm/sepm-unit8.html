Risks associated with Global Software Development

According to the paper by Verner et al (2014), one of the most commonly identified risks while working off-shore teams is the know-how gap against on-shore. It comes with associated costs that might not become apparent until the project is in an advanced stage. Even when local teams of developers are usually multicultural, the fact that they live in the same country where the project is being developed, might facilitate some communication, and could affect the outcome when the complexity of the project is high (Verner et al., 2014).

Other risks include communication problems due to language or cultural differences, and time-zone disparities; bad cost estimation due to currency changes or difficulties to keep the same team members during the project; scope creep or difficulties to have a well-defined scope during the project, which can derive in higher costs, mostly due to bad communication channels or lack of experience of project management.

Wiki Entry: Risks and risk mitigation

This term refers to a project that goes beyond the scope, growing without control, causing cost and time issues. It is a risk associated with complex projects that is more common in Global Software Development or off-shore. Some causes can include difficulty communicating effectively between team members or with the client, lack of management experience and bad design.

One way to mitigate this risk is to use an experienced team for the task, clearly defining the project scope from the beginning, involving all the stakeholders to avoid or reduce misunderstandings.p>

Tutor's question

"If you didn't have an experienced team how else would you mitigate this element of the risk of scope creep?"

One way to decrease scope creep chances is involving end-users so that they can participate from the beginning in communicating their requirements and giving feedback on deployed features.

Another measure is to work on developers and stakeholders education. It is critical to make sure that team members know exactly the scope of the project and the time available for each task. This approach helps to avoid Gold Plating. This term is related to features or improvements that the team add to achieve perfection, even though they have not been required by the users. These features can cause delays or increase project complexity, thus creating scope creep, and there is no guarantee that they will increase customer satisfaction.
